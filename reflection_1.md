## Reflection Pt.2: General Conclusion(s)
  Going into this project, I wasn't sure how much of hte personalization idea would actually be achievable. It's a nice ideal to aspire to, but I doubted my ability to create a tool that actually creates curricula catered to individual learners -- and an evaluation framework that assesses its ability to do so. What I'm most proud of is that the pipeline actually functions and tunes its result(s) to students' personal, varying needs. A student can have a real conversation with the coach, take a quiz that references their specific interests (therefore keeping them engaged), and receive a lesson plan that's tailored to them. I was very impressed by how well the interest hooks turned out, and was impressed when I got to watch the model make specific connections, like linking media literacy to Marcus' Tiktok use and framing online safety in basketball terms. This reassured me that the system was successfully doing what it was designed to do. 

  I would consider the evaluation framework to be the most unexpectedly useful part of the project. I went into stage #4 assuming it would just be a quick, final verification step, but navigating challenges -- like building and iterating the scorecard criteria -- forced me to consider what success looked like for each stage of my main pipeline, which ultimately motivated me to go back and tighten the prompts. The fifth crietia I added, which checked whether the quiz integrated the student's interests in practice, came directly out of this process, and I likely wouldn't have thought to check for it if I hadn't been building the evaluator alongside my main pipeline and putting deliberate thought into what needed to be evaluated. 

  The biggest thing that did not work as well as I had expected it to was the stage 1 conversation loop. Figuring out how to get the model to hold a natural back-and-fourth conversation and then know when to stop asking questions and output the collected student data took much more iteration than I had expected. The version where extract_json failed because the model was waiting for input that couldn't be entered was slightly frustrating, but I also should have anticipated the issue earlier on, rather than discovering it in the middle of my process. While I should have put more consideration into the difference between a single-call prompt and an interactive loop, I ultimately got the pipeline to where I need to. 

  If I were to go through the process of creating this tool again, I would want to build the stage 1 conversation loop first instead of saving it for later, because it's the most structually different part of the pipeline and adding it at the end of my process is what caused a large portion of my issues. I would also want to test the model on more than three personas because while my evaluation framework is functional, it's only as good as the range of students it gets tested on. Using 5-6 (or more) personas with more edge cases would give me a lot more assurance that the system is truly robust and workable.

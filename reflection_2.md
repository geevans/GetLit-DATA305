## Reflection Pt. 2: Ethical Analysis

  The most apparent concern I have with my tool is the bias that is inherently baked into it. While this came up in a variety of ways, a prime example is its inference of students' comfort levels. It turned Jordan's response to the question "When you run into a tech problem, what do you usually do?" -- "I usually just Google it or ask my older sister" -- into the label of "beginner", making a judgement call based on language patterns in the model's data. That data reflects a specifc understanding and assumptions about what it looks like to be tech-proficient, meaning a student (like Jordan) who describes their relationship with technology casually could get thrown into the beginner category because their self-expression doesn't match what the model expects. All other profile features aside, Jordan may actually be good with technology, but because he described his skills this way, was misrepresented and misgrouped by the model. Although it's efficient, this is a substantial issue with the inferred label, as it affects every other decision for the rest of the pipeline. If it's biased, other features like quiz difficulty, lesson framing, priority order (etc.) could also be biased, and this bias can easily be compounded if left unaddressed. 

  Another notable problem is the representation gap in the evaluation framework. My three test personas (Jordan, Sarah, and Marcus) are supposed to cover a range of literacy levels and interests, but at the end of the day I constructed them, meaning my biases are essentially injected into each of their profiles. By choosing their grades, hobbies, and comfort levels I was testing whether my pipeline worked for students who look like how I imagine users of this tool would look. Any student that falls outside of my perception of this is neglected and isn't represented in my evaluation, meaning I can't be sure that my model would perform well for them. 

  Finally, there's the issue of privacy that naturally arises when creating a tool that is meant for students/minors, or any human user for that matter. Stage 1 collects students' names, grade levels, and interests and this data is then used in every later stage. If this tool were to realistically be deployed on students, this would raise questions as to where the data goes, who can see it, and how long it is stored for. Currently, the pipeline doesn't really do anything to mitigate or even acknowledge this, and because the model is (or is supposed to be) generating content based on real student inputs, there's a hallucination risk that could have serious consequences. If the model were to misjudge someone's reading level or make some other un-based assumption, it could have major implications for the type of curriculum a student receives -- and more importantly, it could alter how a students views themself as a learner, which could counteract the tool's very purpose.
